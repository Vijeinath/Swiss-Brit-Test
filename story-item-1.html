bound to Basel against my will

Johann Jacob Frey 1606-1636

Johann Jacob Frey was born as the only son of a notable Basel family. His parent brought up the “little plant” very carefully and a 16 years of age he obtained a special permission to attend public lectures at the university. His father had died in the same year, and from then on, the gifted young man Johann Jacob was considered a consolation for his “afflicted mother”.

Nevertheless, Frey departed for the iter literarium, the study tour which many yougn Swiss students undertook. Having graduated MA from Basel at age 21, he went to study in Geneva, Lyons and Oxford, where he signed the Bodleian Library’s user book in 1627. He was incorporated MA of Oxford as a member of Christ Church on 4 July 1629, and ordained as Anglican Deacon at Westminster in May 1630. This must have meant that he spoke and wrote English well enough to preach by that time, and this also enabled him to correspond with women (who did not usually know Latin) and to exchange English letters with scholars who usually used Latin.

From January 1629, Frey was tutor to Richard, Viscount Dungarvan, the oldest son of the Earl of Cork, He was in Basel as pastor at St. Margrethen for a few months and briefly matriculated for theology in Leyden. In 1632-33, he toured France with young Dungarvan, then stayed in London and Lismore, doing research for James Ussher.

In the summer of 1635, after Dungarvan’s marriage, Frey returned to Basel as Professor of Greek, although he felt “bound to Basel against my will”. The books he brought home include sermons and theology, but also poetry, plays, Ralegh's History of the world, and a Second Folio of Shakespeare’s works. These volumes are the basis of the “Anglica” in the Frey-Grynaeum library in Basel, a building and collection established by the theology professor Johann Ludwig Frey, a great-grandson (1682-1759).

Back in Basel, Frey turned down a pressing offer to tutor the young semi-orphaned sons of the Duke of Buckingham; the young aristocrats were then brought up at the court of Charles I. Fery’s hope to return to Britain and spend the rest of his life there came close to fulfilment when he was offered the Deanery of Armagh in Northern Ireland. He was planning to move back in the spring 1637 but died of the plague in August 1636, just 30 years old, on the very day when the “Rat” of Basel decided to let him go.

Frey was intensely mourned by Swiss and English friends as a brilliantly gifted and lovable man. As late as 1653, Archbishop James Ussher fondly remembered the intellectual exchanges he enjoyed when “our Frey was among the living”. The few hundred letters of his English correspondence are a unique corpus among the ca. 50’000 17th- and 18th-century letters which are kept in the Iinversity Library of Basel.
